1. sxdm (Advanced Photon Source Focus)
Given that your macro utilizes Bluesky-style commands (bps.mv) commonly used at U.S. synchrotron facilities, the open-source sxdm library is highly applicable. It was explicitly built to analyze SXDM and X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) framesets, particularly those generated at the 26-ID-C nanoprobe beamline at the Advanced Photon Source . The library provides a Python toolkit to directly import these specific multidimensional datasets, track the Bragg peak centroids across the multiple incident $\theta$ angles, and extract local strain maps without requiring you to build the geometry from scratch .
2. XSOCS and id01-sxdm-utils (ESRF Focus)
Developed primarily at the European Synchrotron Radiation Facility (ESRF), XSOCS (Strain and Orientation Calculation Software) and its associated id01-sxdm-utils package are designed to handle 5D datasets where a 2D detector frame is recorded at each samx/samy spatial point across an extra motor dimension (like your theta rocking curve) . The software automatically bins the raw diffracted intensity data to construct 3D reciprocal space maps and then computationally separates the lattice tilt from the microstrain to generate highly resolved 2D spatial maps .
3. darfix
If you are generating massive, memory-intensive detector arrays at each spatial pixel, darfix is a highly optimized Python package for treating rocking curve imaging (RCI) and dark-field X-ray microscopy data . It supports operations on larger-than-memory image sets to calculate 3D maps of lattice strain and orientation, and it offers both a Python library interface and a workflow graphical user interface (GUI) .
4. Phase Retrieval Packages (bcdi, Phaser, bonsu)
If your $\theta$ steps are fine enough to highly oversample the coherent diffraction patterns, you can transition into Bragg Coherent Diffraction Imaging (BCDI). Open-source Python packages like bcdi, bonsu, and Phaser are designed to take the stacked 2D intensity datasets across your rocking curve and mathematically invert them using iterative phase-retrieval algorithms . This allows you to reconstruct the exact 3D real-space morphology and internal strain field of the individual diffracting crystals .
Using these libraries, you can bypass the need to write custom center-of-mass tracking algorithms and directly convert your raw, multi-angle detector images into quantifiable metrics for crystalline mosaicity and lattice deformation.
